Watching him die of ALS the same way I will was the most painful thing I’ve ever experienced.

You see, I inherited the most common genetic cause of Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS) and Frontotemporal Dementia (FTD) from him. Every missed step, every lost gesture, every night repositioning him multiple times an hour. The agony in his eyes when he couldn’t breathe, yet blamed it on a mispositioned face mask. Progression to death in 14 months, faster than we could have imagined. In his eyes—our shared hazel eyes—I saw my future laid out before me.

I’ve said I’m trying to cure ALS to save my family, but that’s only half the truth. I’m trying to cure ALS before it gets me.

I didn’t make the rules, I had to learn them. I exist within a sick numbers game, a hidden timer started the day I was born. Heads: 50% chance it hits in 20 years, like my aunt. Tails: 95% in 30, like my dad. I’m trying to outrun the clock while waiting for the shoe to drop. I’m trying to prevent C9orf72 ALS and FTD in my own life before these diseases make it impossible to have a fruitful career as a physician-scientist. Earlier this year, I decided that instead of leveraging my career to target other incurable diseases, I would start by trying to survive.

I miss my dad every single day. His exuberant predisposition. Our intertwined hands. Our last full-bodied hug. What makes his loss more devastating on the first anniversary of his death is knowing the story of ALS in my family doesn’t end here. It’ll repeat in my generation and those that follow unless we do something about it.

This isn’t the path I would have chosen, but it’s the path I’ve been given.

I wrote about this journey anonymously earlier this year. In that process, I decided to stop trying to outrun my genetic predisposition to ALS & FTD, and turn around to meet it head-on. In 6 months, I’ve given samples to 3 ALS/FTD clinical trials, attended 6 neurodegeneration conferences, had 50+ meetings with experts, and developed 6 C9 projects to this end.

“Will you help me cure myself?” or “You’ve tried this in Alzheimer’s, but have you considered ALS?” or “How can I help you achieve ⸻ in the ALS/FTD space?” Being direct is my secret weapon. It’s not about publications, awards, or prestige. All hands on deck—I win by surviving. And I’ll bring 200,000+ patients with me.

I’m in battle mode, but I can’t do it alone. If you’re fighting ALS/FTD in any capacity—in the lab, in the clinic, in the boardroom, through philanthropy, in your own life or in your family—let’s talk. There are innumerable ways to join the fight for survival.
